📌 **What is “Objective Reality”?**

**Definition:**  
👉 **Objective reality** refers to _what exists independently of anyone’s thoughts, beliefs, feelings, or perceptions._

*   Example: The existence of the sun, the force of gravity, or the fact that water is H₂O — these don’t depend on what you _think_ about them.

* * *

🔬 **Everyday Example**
-----------------------

✅ **Objective reality:**

*   A rock has mass. If you drop it, it falls to the ground.
*   This is true whether you believe in gravity or not.

🚫 **Subjective reality:**

*   Whether you _like_ the rock, _what you name it_, or _what you think it symbolizes_ is subjective.

* * *

🧠 **Why Do We Talk About It?**
-------------------------------

Because humans interpret reality through subjective lenses:

*   **Senses:** We can misperceive things (e.g., optical illusions).
*   **Culture & language:** Influence how we describe reality.
*   **Bias:** Cognitive and emotional filters shape our “truths.”

So, philosophers separate:

*   **The thing itself** (_objective_).
*   **Our perception of it** (_subjective_).

📌 **What is “Objective Moral Truth”?**
---------------------------------------

**Definition:**  
👉 An **objective moral truth** is the idea that some actions are right or wrong **independent of what anyone thinks, feels, or believes** about them.

In other words:  
✅ They’re true for _everyone, everywhere, at all times_.  
🚫 They don’t change based on opinion, culture, or personal preference.

* * *

🗝️ **Simple Example**
----------------------

*   **Objective moral truth:** “It’s wrong to torture innocent people for fun.”
    *   Even if a culture says it’s okay, or an individual likes it — it’s still wrong.
*   **Subjective moral belief:** “It’s rude to wear hats indoors.”
    *   This depends on cultural norms or personal opinion.

* * *

📚 **Where Does This Idea Come From?**
--------------------------------------

This is a cornerstone of **moral realism** — the view that:

1.  Moral statements (_“Murder is wrong”_) describe real facts about the world.
2.  These facts are true or false regardless of what anyone thinks.
3.  We can discover them, just like we discover scientific facts.

* * *

🧩 **Examples of Objective Moral Theories**
-------------------------------------------

Here are a few ways philosophers have argued for objective moral truths:

### ✅ **1\. Natural Law (Aristotle, Aquinas)**

*   Morality is based on human nature.
*   Example: Humans naturally seek flourishing; actions that violate this (e.g., killing the innocent) are objectively wrong.

### ✅ **2\. Kantian Ethics**

*   Kant argued that certain actions are morally wrong because they violate universal moral laws you _couldn’t will for everyone_.
*   Example: Lying is always wrong because you can’t universalize it without contradiction.

### ✅ **3\. Utilitarianism (Some versions)**

*   Some utilitarians say maximizing well-being is objectively good: More happiness = better. Less suffering = worse.
*   The metric (pain/pleasure) is treated as a universal standard.

### ✅ **4\. Human Rights**

*   Many people believe that rights like “freedom from slavery” or “right to life” are universal — i.e., objectively true, regardless of culture or era.

* * *

🔍 **Why Do People Doubt Objective Morality?**
----------------------------------------------

1.  **Cultural Relativism:** Different cultures have conflicting moral codes — so who decides?
2.  **Moral Disagreement:** Deep disagreements suggest morality might be subjective.
3.  **No Moral ‘Atoms’:** Unlike physical facts (like molecules), you can’t point to “moral facts” with a microscope.
4.  **Evolutionary Explanations:** Some argue morality evolved to help us cooperate — not because it’s objectively true.

* * *

⚖️ **Key Distinction**
----------------------

| Aspect | Objective Moral Truth | Subjective Moral Belief |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Depends on opinion? | 🚫 No | ✅ Yes |
| Same for everyone? | ✅ Yes | 🚫 No |
| Can change? | 🚫 No | ✅ Yes |
| Example | “Murder is wrong” | “Pineapple on pizza is wrong” |

* * *

🗨️ **Bottom Line**
-------------------

✅ **Objective moral truths** claim that some actions are _universally_ right or wrong.  
✅ This is a big debate: Moral realists defend it, moral relativists deny it.  
✅ It overlaps with the idea that some values (like human dignity) transcend time and culture.

Here’s how AI researchers typically approach this:

* * *

### ✅ **1\. Fixed Rule-Based Systems**

**How it works:**

*   Encode explicit “objective” moral rules as logic constraints.
*   The AI checks its possible actions against these rules.
*   If an action breaks a rule → it’s blocked or flagged.

**Example:**

```

    reject_action()
```

**When used:**

*   In safety-critical systems (e.g., self-driving cars shouldn’t run people over, period).

**Limitation:**

*   Real-world scenarios are messy. What counts as “harm”? What if trade-offs exist?

* * *

### ✅ **2\. Utility Functions (Consequentialist/Utilitarian)**

**How it works:**

*   Define a “goodness” metric: happiness, well-being, safety, etc.
*   The AI chooses actions that maximize this score.
*   You can treat this metric as an “objective standard.”

**Example:**

```
score = happiness_generated - suffering_caused
choose_action_with_max_score()
```

**When used:**

*   Content recommendation (maximize engagement — but this shows why you have to align the metric carefully!)
*   AI alignment research tries to encode human flourishing instead of just clicks.

**Limitation:**

*   Hard to measure well-being precisely. Can lead to weird unintended incentives (like addictive recommendations).

* * *

### ✅ **3\. Learning from Human Feedback (Subjective/Inter-subjective)**

**How it works:**

*   Use human ratings or demos to teach the AI what’s “right.”
*   The system adjusts its behavior to align with human preferences.
*   This can adapt to different cultures or contexts.

**Example:**

*   ChatGPT was trained with Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).

**When used:**

*   General-purpose chatbots, moral decision helpers.

**Limitation:**

*   The AI reflects human biases and disagreements. It can’t discover “objective moral truths” on its own.

* * *

### ✅ **4\. Hybrid Approaches**

**How it works:**

*   Combine fixed guardrails (objective constraints) with flexible learning (subjective preferences).
*   E.g., “Never cause physical harm to humans” + “Adapt tone to user’s cultural context.”

**Example in design:**

*   Big language models use safety layers with hard rules (e.g., don’t incite violence) and soft rules (e.g., adjust politeness).

* * *

📚 **Philosophy in Practice**
-----------------------------

In practice:

*   **Objective moral truths → encoded as non-negotiable constraints.**
    *   e.g., “Never intentionally kill innocent people.”
*   **Subjective norms → encoded as adjustable or learned from context.**

*   How carefully you balance hard constraints vs. flexibility.

*   **Feedback loop:** Regulators or society revise the rules as norms shift.
