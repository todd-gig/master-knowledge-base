* * *

What Ethnographic Research Is
-----------------------------

Ethnographic research is a qualitative, immersive methodology where researchers observe and interact with people in their natural environments to understand:

*   What they do (actual behavior vs. stated behavior)
*   Why they do it (motivations, constraints, beliefs)
*   How they navigate real-world workflows, tools, and decision points
*   Social dynamics, cultural norms, and contextual variables that shape action

This intel exposes:

*   Latent needs
*   Workarounds
*   Hidden frictions
*   Mental models
*   Trigger conditions that precede decisions

From a Gigaton lens, it’s the front-end asset that maps the **human cognitive-operational stack**—the substrate required to engineer human-tech co-evolution systems that actually perform.

* * *

How It Increases Prediction Accuracy of Next Actions
----------------------------------------------------

Ethnography improves mathematical prediction because it allows you to formalize the causal architecture behind behavior.

### 1\. Converts qualitative insights into structured variables

Ethnographic observations become:

*   State variables
*   Trigger events
*   Conditional dependencies
*   Behavioral probabilities
*   Decision pathways

This transforms fuzzy human behavior into **model-ready data**.

### 2\. Identifies causal drivers instead of correlations

Machine learning typically sees:

*   “When X happens, Y tends to follow.”

Ethnography reveals:

*   “Y only happens when X + belief B + constraint C align.”

This increases model precision and minimizes false positives.

### 3\. Enables construction of **behavioral state machines**

Ethnography provides the raw material to build deterministic or probabilistic state machines:

*   Current state → Trigger → Expected next action
*   With real-world confidence intervals

These outperform generic ML classification models in operational environments.

### 4\. Enables segmentation by _mental models_ instead of demographics

People with the same demographic profile behave differently.  
People with the same mental model behave predictably.

Ethnography clusters users by:

*   Values
*   Decision frameworks
*   Risk tolerance
*   Meaning structures
*   Emotional triggers
*   Friction patterns

These cognitive archetypes drive much stronger next-action prediction.

### 5\. Provides calibration data for causal graphs

Ethnography supplies the “ground truth” edges in a Bayesian or structural causal model.

Examples:

*   Users don’t click “buy” because of price, but because the product reduces uncertainty.
*   Users always delay decision until peer validation is confirmed.

These micro-rules radically increase predictive power.

### 6\. Reduces noise in the model

Quantitative datasets are polluted with:

*   Spurious correlations
*   Incomplete features
*   Ambiguous signals

Ethnography helps identify which variables matter and which are noise.  
This boosts prediction accuracy and reduces computational overhead.

### 7\. Powers hybrid models (qual → quant → ML)

The highest ROI path is:

1.  Ethnography identifies the causal features
2.  Behavioral logic is translated into quantifiable model features
3.  ML operationalizes those features at scale
4.  Continuous ethnographic feedback refines the model

This creates a **closed-loop predictive optimization engine.**

0\. Frame the Causal Question
-----------------------------

1.  Define the target outcome(s)
    *   Example: “Probability user books a stay in next 7 days”

2.  Define decision context

3.  Define levers you can actually change
    *   Offers, messaging, UI flow, timing, follow-ups, agent actions, etc.
    *   If you can’t intervene on it, it’s a context variable, not a lever.

Output:

*   A 1–2 line **outcome definition** and a **short list of controllable levers**.

* * *

1\. Design Ethnography to Expose Causal Structure
-------------------------------------------------

You’re not just “observing people”; you’re hunting for:

*   Triggers: “What happened right before they did X?”
*   Decision rules: “In their own words, how do they choose?”
*   Constraints: Time, trust, money, knowledge, social approval
*   Workarounds / hacks: Reveal hidden goals and friction
*   Thresholds: “Only do it when…” or “Never do it if…”

Tactics:

*   Contextual inquiry: Observe real tasks in real environments
*   Think-aloud protocols: Get moment-by-moment decision narration
*   Shadowing: Follow customers across channels and time
*   Critical incident interviews: Dissect specific “I did X” events

Output:

*   Recorded sessions, notes, and incident narratives mapped around the target outcome.

* * *

2\. Code Ethnographic Data into Structured Constructs
-----------------------------------------------------

You turn raw stories into **variables and conditions**.

1.  Open coding (exploratory):
    *   Tag everything: goals, frustrations, triggers, people involved, tools used.
2.  Axial coding (structure):
    *   Group tags into higher-order constructs:
        *   “Trust in source”
        *   “Perceived effort to complete”
        *   “Fear of making a bad choice”
        *   “Need for social validation”
3.  Causal pattern extraction:
    *   Look for consistent sequences:
        *   “When user gets peer reassurance → they proceed quickly”
        *   “When decision feels irreversible + high cost → they stall or abandon”
4.  Turn constructs into **proto-variables**:
    *   Trust level: low / medium / high
    *   Time pressure: none / moderate / high
    *   Social proof present: yes / no
    *   Perceived complexity: numeric scale, but grounded in real quotes/behavior

Output:

*   Codebook: list of constructs, definitions, and example quotes
*   Candidate variables with clear operational meaning.

* * *

3\. Build the Causal Graph (Qualitative First)
----------------------------------------------

Now you formalize “who causes what.”

1.  Define nodes:
    *   Outcome node(s): e.g., “Book stay,” “Upgrade plan,” “Drop from funnel”
    *   Latent nodes from ethnography: trust, anxiety, clarity, effort, status, etc.
    *   Observables: page views, clicks, time on task, channel, agent action.
2.  Draw edges based on ethnographic logic:
    *   Example snippet:
        *   “Agent response speed” → “Perceived respect” → “Trust” → “Commitment to proceed”
        *   “Number of options shown” → “Choice overload” → “Decision delay” → “Dropoff”
3.  Identify mediators and moderators:
    *   Mediator: “Social validation” mediates effect of “Discount offered” → “Purchase”
    *   Moderator: “Tech savvy” changes the effect size of “UI complexity” on “Dropoff”
4.  Encode assumptions explicitly:
    *   Document: “We assume trust must be medium+ before discount has strong effect”
    *   This is what you later test and refine.

Output:

*   DAG or SCM sketch: nodes and directed edges, in a tool or diagram.
*   List of causal hypotheses to test: “If we increase X, Y should change via Z.”

* * *

4\. Translate Constructs into Measurable Features
-------------------------------------------------

You now make it **machine-readable**.

1.  Map constructs → observable proxies:
    *   Trust →
        *   N prior positive interactions
        *   Response time consistency
        *   Self-reported NPS/CSAT
        *   Repeat visit count
    *   Social proof →
        *   “Friends have booked” indicator
        *   Visible reviews or rating exposure
        *   Time spent on testimonial/review sections
2.  Define feature engineering recipes:
    *   Binary: “Social proof viewed” (yes/no)
    *   Categorical: “Decision style archetype” (fast decider, maximizer, avoider)
    *   Continuous: “Decision latency” (time between info shown and action)
    *   Interaction terms: “High effort × low trust” as a combined risk feature
3.  Design instrumentation plan:
    *   What to log in product / IVR / WhatsApp / CRM
    *   Event names, properties, IDs, timestamps
    *   How to stitch cross-channel behavior into one user journey

Output:

*   Feature spec document: each field = name, type, calculation, data source
*   Tracking plan ready for engineers and data teams.

* * *

5\. Quantify the Causal Model
-----------------------------

Now you move from just “beliefs” to **parameters**.

1.  Select modeling approach:
    *   Bayesian network or probabilistic graphical model
    *   Structural equation model (SEM)
    *   Causal forest / DoWhy / other causal ML frameworks
    *   Or: rules-plus-ML hybrid (e.g., hand-crafted state machine + classifier)
2.  Use historical + newly instrumented data:
    *   Estimate conditional probabilities:
        *   P(next\_action | trust\_level, social\_proof, time\_pressure, …)
    *   Fit parameters for edges in your DAG:
        *   Strength and sign of each link, confidence intervals.
3.  Causal validation:
    *   Backdoor checks, common cause identification
    *   Sensitivity analysis: if we tweak “trust” distribution, do predicted outcomes move in line with qualitative expectations?
4.  Encode decision rules:
    *   Example:
        *   If P(book in 7 days | current state) > 0.7 → push upsell
        *   If P(dropoff | high friction, low trust) > 0.5 → route to human agent

Output:

*   Calibrated causal model with tested parameters
*   A mapping from model state → next-best action policy.

2.  Build an action policy layer:
    *   Each state maps to candidate actions with ranked utility:
        *   Send reassurance info
        *   Provide social proof
        *   Offer a flexible/no-risk option
        *   Route to senior agent
        *   Offer discount vs. hold price
3.  Execution:
    *   Expose via API to:

1\. INTENT CATEGORIES (Top-Level Taxonomy)
==========================================

Every inbound contact falls into one of the following macro-intents. Each intent drives a different sales motion, risk profile, and revenue potential.

A. Immediate Booking Intent
---------------------------

**Definition:** Customer is in an active decision window (<72 hours).  
**Indicators:**

*   Specific dates, number of guests, property type
*   Questions about availability, payment, cancellation
*   Time-sensitive language

**Examples of language:**

*   “Is this property available from the 12th to 15th?”
*   “Can I book now?”
*   “What’s the total cost if I confirm today?”

**Qualification rules:**

*   Provides dates without prompting
*   Answers price questions quickly
*   Accepts alternatives when given ±1 day
*   Low tolerance for delays → route to high-speed close

* * *

B. Planning / Research Intent
-----------------------------

**Definition:** Customer is exploring options but not ready to transact.  
**Goal:** Move from uncertainty → clarity → commitment.

**Examples of language:**

*   “Just comparing places right now.”
*   “How far is this from the beach?”
*   “Do you have recommendations for first-time visitors?”

**Qualification rules:**

*   Doesn’t provide dates until asked
*   Asks for lists rather than specifics
*   Engages in open-ended questions
*   High value for nurturing sequences (drip → trust build → timed reactivation)

* * *

C. Price Shopping Intent
------------------------

**Definition:** Customer is minimizing cost, optimizing deals, or anchoring price.  
**Examples:**

*   “What’s your best price?”
*   “Airbnb shows a lower price—can you match?”
*   “Do you offer discounts?”

**Qualification rules:**

*   Avoids committing to dates
*   Compares platforms
*   Reacts strongly to fees, taxes, total cost
*   Convert with price framing, flexible deposit, and structured scarcity

-------------------------------------

**Definition:** Customer wants to book but perceives risk or uncertainty.

**Examples:**

*   “Is this safe to book?”

**Qualification rules:**

*   Looks for validation
*   Seeks reversibility (free cancellation, hold options)

*   Prioritize trust-building content → social proof → escalated human engagement
